Module 1: Introduction to HIPAA

What is HIPAA?

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) is a federal law that protects sensitive patient health information from being disclosed without the patient's consent or knowledge.

HIPAA's Three Main Rules

  • Privacy Rule (45 CFR Part 164, Subpart E): Establishes national standards for the protection of PHI and gives patients rights over their health information
  • Security Rule (45 CFR Part 164, Subpart C): Sets standards for protecting electronic PHI (ePHI) through administrative, physical, and technical safeguards
  • Breach Notification Rule (45 CFR Part 164, Subpart D): Requires notification to individuals, HHS, and sometimes media when unsecured PHI is breached
Trainer Note: "HIPAA applies to every single one of us. Whether you're a doctor, a receptionist, or work in IT â€“ if you have access to patient information, HIPAA applies to you."

Module 3: Understanding Protected Health Information (PHI)

What is PHI?

Protected Health Information (PHI) is any information about health status, provision of health care, or payment for health care that can be linked to a specific individual.

The 18 HIPAA Identifiers

PHI includes health information combined with any of these 18 identifiers:

  1. Names
  2. Geographic data (smaller than state)
  3. Dates (except year) related to individual
  4. Phone numbers
  5. Fax numbers
  6. Email addresses
  7. Social Security numbers
  8. Medical record numbers
  9. Health plan beneficiary numbers
  10. Account numbers
  11. Certificate/license numbers
  12. Vehicle identifiers
  13. Device identifiers
  14. Web URLs
  15. IP addresses
  16. Biometric identifiers
  17. Full-face photos
  18. Any other unique identifier
Trainer Note: "If in doubt whether something is PHI, treat it as PHI and protect it accordingly. It's better to over-protect than under-protect."

Module 5: Permitted Uses and Disclosures

PHI May Be Used/Disclosed WITHOUT Authorization For:

  • Treatment: Providing, coordinating, or managing health care
  • Payment: Billing, claims processing, collection activities
  • Health Care Operations: Quality improvement, training, audits
  • To the Individual: Patients can access their own records
  • As Required by Law: Court orders, public health reporting

PHI REQUIRES Written Authorization For:

  • Marketing purposes
  • Sale of PHI
  • Most uses of psychotherapy notes
  • Disclosures to employers for employment decisions
  • Any purpose not otherwise permitted

Remember: When in doubt, get authorization in writing!

Module 6: Patient Rights Under HIPAA

Patients have the following rights that you must respect:

  • Right to Access: View and obtain copies of their records (respond within 30 days)
  • Right to Amend: Request corrections to their records
  • Right to Accounting: Receive a list of certain disclosures
  • Right to Restrictions: Request limits on how their PHI is used
  • Right to Confidential Communications: Request alternative contact methods
  • Right to Notice: Receive our Notice of Privacy Practices
  • Right to Complain: File complaints without retaliation

Scenario: Phone Inquiry

Situation: Someone calls claiming to be a patient's spouse and asks about their condition.

Correct Action: Verify their identity. Check if the patient has authorized this person to receive information. If unsure, take a message and have the patient's care team call back after verification.

Module 7: Security Safeguards

Physical Safeguards

  • Lock your computer when leaving your desk (Windows: Win+L, Mac: Ctrl+Cmd+Q)
  • Position monitors away from public view
  • Secure paper records in locked cabinets
  • Use cover sheets on faxes containing PHI
  • Dispose of PHI properly (shredding, secure bins)
  • Challenge strangers in restricted areas

Technical Safeguards

  • Use strong, unique passwords (12+ characters)
  • Never share your login credentials
  • Encrypt emails containing PHI
  • Don't store PHI on personal devices or cloud accounts
  • Report suspicious emails (phishing) immediately
  • Only use approved software and applications

Administrative Safeguards

  • Complete all required training
  • Follow all policies and procedures
  • Report incidents immediately
  • Verify identity before disclosing PHI

Scenario: Computer Left Unlocked

Situation: You see a coworker's computer unlocked with patient records visible while they're away.

Correct Action: Lock the computer immediately (Win+L) and remind your coworker about HIPAA security requirements when they return.

Module 9: Incidental Disclosures

Some incidental disclosures are permitted if reasonable safeguards are in place. However, you must still minimize them.

Examples of Permitted Incidental Disclosures:

  • A conversation overheard despite using lowered voices
  • A patient's name visible on a sign-in sheet (if minimized)

Examples of NOT Permitted (Avoidable) Disclosures:

  • Discussing patient information loudly in hallways
  • Leaving patient records visible on a desk
  • Talking about patients in elevators or cafeterias

Module 10: Breach Identification and Reporting

What is a Breach?

A breach is the unauthorized acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of PHI that compromises its security or privacy.

Examples of Breaches:

  • Laptop containing PHI is stolen
  • Mailing PHI to the wrong patient
  • Employee accesses records without authorization
  • Ransomware attack on systems containing PHI
  • Improperly disposed records found in trash
  • PHI shared with wrong recipient via email/fax

Module 13: Personal Representatives and Special Situations

Personal Representatives

A personal representative has the same rights as the patient regarding PHI. Examples include:

  • Parent of a minor child (with exceptions)
  • Legal guardian
  • Healthcare power of attorney
  • Executor of deceased patient's estate

Minors

Generally, parents are personal representatives for minors. Exceptions may apply for:

  • Emancipated minors
  • Minors who consent to their own treatment under state law
  • Situations where parental access could endanger the minor

Deceased Patients

PHI of deceased individuals remains protected. Disclosures may be made to personal representatives of the estate or family members involved in care, consistent with the deceased's prior expressed preferences.
